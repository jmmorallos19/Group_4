Florante at Laura (1838) Written by Francisco Balagtas in 1838, Florante at Laura was heavily influenced by the social and political climate of the Philippines during Spanish colonization. Though the poem is set in a fictionalized version of Albania, it serves as an allegory for the injustices and oppression experienced by Filipinos under Spanish rule. During this period, the Spanish colonial government exerted strict control over the Philippines, and Filipino natives, particularly the lower class or Indios, suffered from discrimination, social inequality, and the abuses of the colonial authorities and friars. Balagtas subtly criticized these injustices through the characters in Florante at Laura—Florante represents the oppressed, while the antagonists (such as Adolfo) symbolize the corrupt ruling powers. The poem’s use of Tagalog was a significant act of resistance. At a time when Spanish was the language of power, writing in Tagalog allowed Balagtas to reach a wider Filipino audience, promoting national identity and pride. The themes of tyranny, love for country, and moral integrity in Florante at Laura reflected the growing Filipino consciousness of the need for social justice and political reform, which eventually contributed to the nationalist movements that followed.

The Pasyon, first popularized in 1704 by Gaspar Aquino de Belen, is a narrative of the passion, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ. Written in poetic form, it became central to Filipino religious life, particularly during Holy Week, when communities gather to chant or sing the Pasyon. Spanish colonization profoundly influenced both its themes and language, as the Spanish missionaries sought to embed Christian doctrine into Filipino culture. The Pasyon was not only a religious text but also a tool for reinforcing colonial control. By spreading the story of Christ's suffering and sacrifice, the Spanish used the themes of redemption and salvation to instill Catholic values among Filipinos. The use of local languages made it more accessible, ensuring widespread adoption. Over time, the Pasyon became deeply ingrained in Filipino spirituality, symbolizing both the internalization of colonial religious teachings and the resilience of Filipino traditions, as it was often performed in communal settings that blended local and colonial practices.

SENAKULO Filipino living the event of the past , Senakulo performances involve actors playing the key figures of the Passion story, such as Jesus, Mary, the apostles, and the Roman soldiers. The play typically follows the scriptural accounts of the last days of Jesus' life, highlighting events like the Last Supper, the agony in the garden, the trial before Pontius Pilate, the crucifixion, and the resurrection.

Doctrina Christiana (1593) - Historical Background: Doctrina Christiana was the first book printed in the Philippines, produced using the woodblock printing method. It was a religious manual, written in Spanish and Tagalog (using the Baybayin script), meant to teach Catholic doctrine to early Filipino converts. Its creation was heavily influenced by the Spanish colonization, which aimed to spread Christianity throughout the islands as part of its imperial strategy. Spanish colonizers, particularly the friars, believed that converting Filipinos to Catholicism would solidify their control over the population. The use of both Spanish and native languages in Doctrina Christiana reflects the colonizers' goal of educating and indoctrinating Filipinos, while also accommodating their need to communicate effectively with the local people. This work highlights the integration of colonial religious teachings into the daily lives of Filipinos, symbolizing both cultural dominance and the beginning of a profound religious transformation that shaped Filipino identity under Spanish rule.
